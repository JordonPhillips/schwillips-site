Location

The wedding will take place at Mildenstein Castle in Leisnig, Germany. The castle has sat on a rocky outcrop above the Mulde River since at least 1046. It's now a museum with well-preserved medieval rooms including the Knight's Hall (where we'll be having the reception) and chapel.

The castle has been carefully restored and is comfortable to visit while maintaining its historical character. The views of the surrounding countryside are gorgeous, and should be particularly beautiful in the fall. We'll be celebrating in spaces that have hosted gatherings for nearly a thousand years!

Accessibility Note: The castle maintains its medieval character, which means there are stairs to reach both the ceremony and reception locations, plus an incline up to the main courtyard. A small freight elevator exists for supplies but cannot accommodate guests. Please let us know in your RSVP if you have mobility concerns.

You will, of course, be able to visit the museum sections of the castle. If you'd like to take part in a guided tour in English (or German), please let us know in your RSVP.

Getting There

The castle is about 2.5 hours from Berlin by car or 3 hours by train. From Leipzig, it's about 1 hour by either method. If arriving by train, note there's an uphill walk from Leisnig station to the castle (about 15 minutes), and taxis may not be readily available.

Non-Wedding Stuff

You may be required to get an ETIAS visa to come from abroad, even as an American. You'll need to do this ahead of time, so check early to see what the requirements are. It's expected to begin in Q4 of 2026, so right in time for the wedding.

When you get here, make sure to get some cash. Lots of places don't take cards at all, so you will need cash.

If you're coming from out of town, you might want to stay in Germany a while to justify the cost of travel. Here's some things to keep in mind.

Public transit in this country is generally excellent. I would recommend downloading the Deutsche Bahn app for long distance transit. If you'll be here for some time it might make sense to buy a "Deutschland Ticket" and then immediately cancel the subscription. That'll get you everything except the high speed, long distance trains (IC and ICE). In Berlin you'll want to download the BVG tickets app. Other cities and areas will have their own systems, which you may be able to pay for through the Deutsche Bahn app. Of course you can also find ticket kiosks at the main train stations if you'd rather get a physical ticket.

If you're booking a long-distance train (IC / ICE), note that a seat is not included with the ticket. You must pay a bit extra to book a seat. You might still get a seat without booking one, but it'll depend on what's free, and you'll have to get up if somebody has a reservation. Medium-distance trains (RC / RE) have no seat reservations, so it's just luck. First class is of course more sparse, but not all trains will have it and it's expensive.

You can eat and drink on medium and long distance trains, but not on short-distance trains, trams, subways, or busses. Also please do not be loud.

There are no ticket gates, but you still need a ticket. People may come around to check your ticket, and if you don't have one you'll get a fine of several hundred euros that you'll have to pay immediately or go to jail. Don't risk it.

Oktoberfest takes place at the end of September/ early October. So if you want to take part in that, you'll need to arrive quite a bit earlier. The main event is in Munich, though there will be celebrations to a lesser extent through the rest of the country. You may need to make early reservations for some places, especially in Munich.

Lots of people in Germany speak English. They start learning it when they're in third grade. That said, younger people generally will be better at it. You don't need to know German to get by, but it can help and it's polite.

If you want to go to other countries, check their entry requirements. You may need a separate visa. Also check what currency they use as not everybody uses the euro. Poland, for example, uses their own currency. Consider using sleeper trains to travel between countries. They're way less hassle than flying, if slower. And better for the environment.
